## A Reference Point for Politics

The written constitution mainly serves as a reference point for modern-day politics. Providing clear definitions of the structure of the government, distribution of power and the fundamental rights of citizens.

The constitution also limits the government by explicitly explaining the things the government *does not* have the power to do, and protects minority groups.

## Rigidity and Amendments

The constitution also makes it more difficult to take away power or change rules of states, requiring amendments and not simply creating new laws, which is clearly highlighted in 
<Term label="Article V" definition="The section of the Constitution describing how to amend it. It establishes the high thresholds (2/3 of Congress, 3/4 of States)." />.

We can clearly see how it is also relatively difficult to create those amendments; Congress has passed thousands of new laws, but only 27 amendments have been added in the last 200 years.

## The Mission Statement (Preamble)

The <Term label="preamble" definition="The introductory statement of the Constitution's fundamental purposes and guiding principles." />, or mission statement, tells us *why* the constitution was written. The mission statement also helps guide politicians and courts when upholding laws, and helps us notice laws that might intrude on those values. 

It also creates a unifying goal, and not separate, dividing goals, which has mostly helped us to share similar goals and values for our country.
